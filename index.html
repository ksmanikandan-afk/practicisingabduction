Abductive Reasoning — Practice Exercises

Three exercises to sharpen your ability to reason toward the best explanation.

Abductive reasoning is the skill of moving from incomplete observations to the most plausible explanation — the engine behind good diagnosis, design thinking, and strategic insight. These three exercises build that skill progressively, from recognising reasoning types to constructing and evaluating your own hypotheses.

You are free to start with any exercise. That said, you will benefit most by going in order — each exercise builds on the thinking developed in the one before it.

Quick Reference — Three Types of Reasoning

Deduction

Moves from a general rule to a specific conclusion. If premises are true, the conclusion must be true.
If A→B, and A is true, then B is true.

Induction

Moves from specific observations to a general conclusion. The conclusion is probable, not guaranteed.
Observed pattern X in cases 1–n → X is likely generally true.

Abduction

The argument to the best explanation. The hypothesis that makes the most sense given observed data or prior experience. Less about "right" — more about "good enough".

Exercise 3 of 3

Build Your Hypotheses

You are given the start and end of a story. Generate at least three hypotheses for what happened in between, then pick the most plausible one. Reflect on your reasoning using the checklist.

Story 1 — The Fallen Brand

Start A well-funded D2C skincare brand had strong sales, a loyal customer base, and consistent 4.5-star reviews across platforms.
18 months later The brand filed for insolvency with ₹4 crore in unsold inventory and mounting debt.

Checklist for Good Abductive Reasoning

  • Best Fit — Does your hypothesis explain all the observed facts together? Both the debt and the unsold inventory? Or just one of them?
  • Parsimony — Is it the simplest explanation that doesn't require extra assumptions? Could you say the same thing in fewer moving parts?
  • Plausibility — Is it grounded in patterns you've seen in the real world — not just something theoretically possible?
  • Testability — What evidence would confirm or challenge your hypothesis? Could you check burn rate data, retail agreements, or customer return rates?
  • Superiority — Is your hypothesis meaningfully better than the alternatives you listed? Does it offer a novel or surprising explanation — one that could point toward a specific, impactful intervention that others might have missed?

Review your most plausible hypothesis against each criterion. How many does it satisfy? Would a different hypothesis on your list score better?

Story 2 — The Late Placer

Start Arjun entered his MBA as a top candidate — strong quant background, compelling work experience, and excellent peer feedback in the first term.
By placement season Arjun was among the last in his batch to receive an offer — and it was below his expectations.

Checklist for Good Abductive Reasoning

  • Best Fit — Does your hypothesis explain the gap between Arjun's strong profile and his weak placement outcome? Or does it only explain part of the story?
  • Parsimony — Does your explanation require assuming many additional factors, or does a single coherent reason cover the gap?
  • Plausibility — Is this a pattern you've seen or heard of in B-school placement contexts? Is it rooted in real experience?
  • Testability — What would you look at to verify this? Mock interview performance, networking activity, application shortlists, peer comparisons?
  • Superiority — Is your hypothesis more illuminating than the obvious ones? A truly strong abductive hypothesis here would not just explain what went wrong but suggest a specific, actionable intervention that others might have missed.

Review your most plausible hypothesis against each criterion. How many does it satisfy? Would a different hypothesis on your list score better?
